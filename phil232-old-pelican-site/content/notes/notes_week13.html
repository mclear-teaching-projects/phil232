Leibniz on Freedom

Incompatibilism
determinism and free will/freedom are incompatible
Compatibilism
determinism and free will/freedom are compatible
  • Leibniz argues for compatibilism based on what a counterpart might do in an alternate possible world (i.e. a world God could have created but didn't)

  • Born in Amsterdam in 1632, dies at The Hague in 1677

The Principle of Sufficient Reason

PSR:
For every fact F, there is a metaphysically sufficient explanation (or reason or ground) why F, rather than not F, is true

Three features of the PSR

  1. Irreflexive: if F explains G then F is not identical to G (i.e. nothing can explain itself)
  2. Asymmetric: If F explains G then G does not explain F
  3. Transitive: If F explains G, and G explains H, then F explains H
  • There are no brute explanations
  • The ground of explanation necessitates (i.e. makes necessary) that which it explains
  • The PSR, applied unrestrictedly, entails determinism

This commitment to the unrestricted use of the PSR means that:

  • Spinoza is not just a determinist but also a necessitarian
  • every state of affairs is necessitated by antecedent states of affairs together with the laws of nature.
  • every truth is necessarily true.

Descartes's & Spinoza's conception of substance

Descartes's official definition of substance in terms of an independent individual:

By substance we can understand nothing other than a thing which exists in such a way as to depend on no other thing for its existence. (Principles, §51)

But then…

Hence the term ‘substance' does not apply univocally, as they say in the Schools, to God and other things; that is, there is no distinctly intelligible meaning of the term which is common to God and his creatures. (Principles, §51)

  • Descartes denies that there is a single sense in which one can apply the term 'substance'

Spinoza adopts the Cartesian definition of substance in terms of independence:

By substance I mean that which is in itself and is conceived through itself; that is, that the conception of which does not require the conception of another thing from which it has to be formed. (E1d3)

Spinoza's Argument for Monism

Spinoza's argument for monism ultimately looks like this:

  1. No two substances share an attribute
  2. God has infinite attributes (i.e. God has all the attributes)
  3. ∴ There can be no substance other than God
  • Step 1 (E1p5): No two substances can share an attribute
  • Step 2 (E1p11): God is a substance with infinite attributes
  • Step 3 (E1p14): God is the only substance
  • Argument for step 1

    Spinoza's argument that no two substances can share an attribute looks like this:

    1. If two substances exist, then they can be distinguished (from the Identity of Indiscernibles)
    2. E1p4: If two substances can be distinguished, then they are distinguished either by a difference in attribute or a difference in mode
    3. No two substances can be distinguished by a difference in mode (from E1d3, E1a6, E1p1)
    4. ∴ Two substances are distinguished by their attributes (from 1, 2, and 3)
    5. If two substances are distinguished by their attributes, then they cannot share an attribute
    6. ∴ Two substances cannot share an attribute
    1. E1d3: Substance is conceived through itself
    2. E1d5: Modes are conceived through something other than the substance to which they belong
    3. E1p1: Therefore, substances are (conceptually) prior to their modes
    4. Substance considered in itself therefore excludes consideration of modes (from A, B & C)
    5. ∴ Substances cannot be distinguished by a difference in their modes (= 3 above)
  • Argument for step 2
    1. E1p7: Existence belongs to the essence of substance
    2. E1d6: By God I understand a being absolutely infinite, that is, a substance consisting of an infinity of attributes, of which each one expresses an eternal and infinite essence
    3. E1p11: ∴ God necessarily exists and has infinite attributes (from E1p7 & E1d6)
  • Third Step
    1. No two substances share an attribute
    2. God has infinite attributes
    3. ∴ There can be no substance other than God

    All finite beings are thus modes of the one – God (see E1p15).

The Project
