Locke

Three features of knowledge

  1. Relation between ideas
  2. Subject must perceive agreement/disagreement between ideas
  3. Knowledge is certain

Different Kinds of Knowledge

  1. Intuitive knowledge
  2. Demonstrative knowledge (anything arrived at via inference)
  3. Sensitive knowledge

Other kinds of epistemic state

  • Belief

    Aims at knowledge but falls short in some way

  • Opinion

    Aims at knowledge but falls even further short of some evidential standard

Can we have scientific knowledge of nature?

  • Corpuscularian hypothesis + Mechanism
    • We can't know that the corpuscularian view is true
      • Obscurity of primary qualities of corpuscles: we can't have knowledge of the real essences of the minute material bodies
      • Inscrutability: even if we could know the real essences of individual particles the connections between primary and secondary qualities are inscrutable to us

Leibniz

Against Matter

  • Cartesian substance
    1. Individuals (subjects of properties)
    2. Independent
  • Matter is not substantial
    1. All space is infinitely divisible
    2. All bodies are extended in space
    3. ∴ All bodies are infinitely divisible
    4. Bodies that are divisible are actually divided – i.e. have parts
    5. What has parts depends for its existence on its parts
    6. ∴ All bodies depend for their existence on their parts
    7. Substance is that which does not depend on anything (except God) for its existence
    8. ∴ No material body is a substance
    • What about atomism? Leibniz denies that there could be material atoms - why?
      1. atoms are arbitrary – the nature of materiality is such as to be infinitely divisible/divided. It would be contrary to reason (and PSR) to stop at some point in the division of matter
      2. atoms are irrelevant – just because they haven't been divided by us doesn't mean that they don't have parts in the relevant sense
  • Matter is not fundamental (not fundamentally real)
    Principle of Borrowed Reality (PBR)
    every being derives its reality only from the reality of those beings of which it is composed
    1. All bodies are aggregates — they are composed of parts
    2. Aggregates borrow their reality from their component parts (PBR)
    3. There cannot be an infinite chain of borrowed realities
    4. ∴ The reality of material bodies cannot itself be explained in material terms
    5. ∴ There exists something else which is not itself material and which is responsible for the existence of body
    6. This argument, if sound, shows that the reality of matter is not something fundamental, but rather must explained in non-material terms.
    7. There are two possible readings of this conclusion, a 'liberal', and an 'austere' reading.
    • Liberal reading

      According to the liberal reading we can say that matter is real but not fundamental—it is rather derived from the non-material in the sense in which the sound of an orchestra is derived from the sounds of the individual players in the orchestra.

    • The austere reading

      The austere reading reserves the term 'real' for those things that are fundamental, so it denies that matter is real, understood as something existing over and above that of the fundamental non-material entities. The analogue here would be to deny the real existence of the sound of the orchestra because there is no sound that really exists over and above the existence of the sounds of each of the individual instruments.

      • What about the importance of the arrangement of the parts making up any particular body? Wouldn't that be a potential source of reality which would end the 'reality regress'?
        • No. Leibniz denies the ultimate reality of relations/relational properties. This means that we cannot find, in the relations that exist between the parts of a thing, some source of reality that might stop the regress.

Leibniz's Idealism

  • The characteristics of substance:
    1. Unity
      • does not have independent parts
    2. Independence
      • does not inhere in anything else
    3. Persistence
      • identity of being through change
    4. Activity
      • a substance is necessarily active or possesses principle of change (for some purpose - final causation)
    5. Individuation
      • a substance has a principle of individuation intrinsic to its nature
  • The characteristics of monads
    1. Simplicity
      • has no parts – is a true unity
    2. Indestuctibility
      • possesses no parts
    3. Immateriality
      • has no parts so cannot be material
    4. Individuality
      • perception
        • various states of the monad, conscious or not
      • appetite
        • principle of succession

    5. 'Windowlessness' (causal isolation/independence)

      • Why must monads be 'windowless'? Leibniz thinks there is no sense to be made of causal interaction between monads. Causal interaction would result either in (i) the rearrangement of parts, but monads don't have parts, or (ii) the transmission of an 'accident' from one thing to another, which Leibniz thinks is incoherent.

      There is also no way of explaining how a monad can be altered or changed internally by some other creature, since one cannot transpose anything in it, nor can one conceive of any internal motion that can be excited, directed, augmented, or diminished within it, as can be done in composites, where there can be change among the parts. The monads have no windows through which something can enter or leave. Accidents cannot be detached, nor can they go about outside of substances, as the sensible species of the Scholastics once did. Thus, neither substance nor accident can enter a monad from without. (ยง7, pp.213-14)

  • The Ideality of Matter
    • Physical objects 'result' from monads
      • monads 'express' bodies via 'perception'
      • physical bodies are a kinds of stable hallucination—a 'well-founded phenomenon'
    • Monads are not literal parts of bodies — rather monads 'express' them. Matter is thus derivative from the perceptions of monads
