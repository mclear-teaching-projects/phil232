Week 14 (4/16)

Hume's Mind

The Project

  • Two aims: positive and negative
    • Positive: The human mind operates on sense impressions and their corresponding ideas, according to principles of association
    • Negative: The pursuit of metaphysical questions is fruitless

Two Kinds of Perception

  • Impressions
  • Ideas

Ideas are less forceful and lively copies of impressions

The Copy Principle

  • All ideas are copies from original impressions
  • If all our ideas must be copied from impressions and our impressions could always change, then how can we be certain that we know anything?

The missing shade of blue

  • Must all ideas be traceable to original impressions?
    • No? Missing shade of blue counterexample
      • Alternative explanations
        • unreliable in figuring out which ideas we have based on our impressions
        • already had the impression but didn't realize it
        • have a complex idea of the shade rather than a simple one
    • Counterexample doesn't matter because it is "so singular"
    • Important because it is relevant to assessing copy principle as a principle determining what thoughts are meaningful

Three Kinds of Association

  1. resemblance
  2. contiguity in space and/or time
  3. cause & effect

Association is the natural means by which the mind relates its ideas and impressions.

Hume on Causation

Hume's Fork: Relations of ideas and matters of fact

  • relations of ideas: truths of mathematics & logic
    • known just via thinking about the proposition, independent of any issue of what exists
    • certain and self-evident
    • necessary truths — i.e. contrary or contradiction is impossible
  • matters of fact: truths of the empirical world
    • denial involves no contradiction
    • contingent rather than necessary
    • known only via experience
    • cannot be arrived at (merely) through logical demonstration

Analytic Truth

Analytic truths are propositions which are true:

  1. in virtue of their negation being a contradiction

ii. in virtue of the predicate being 'contained' in the subject iii. in virtue of the meaning of the constituent concepts/words

A priori Justification & Knowledge

  1. Justification/Knowledge which is derived or possessed independently of experience (i.e. independent of perception, introspection, memory, and perhaps testimony) needed to understand the proposition at issue.

ii. Justification/Knowledge of causes/grounds rather than effects

  • a posteriori knowledge/justification is knowledge that does depend on experience
    • e.g. "It is snowing in Lincoln"

Knowledge of Matters of Fact

  • We have knowledge of occurrent matters of fact via our occurrent experience
  • What gives us knowledge/certainty of anything beyond what we're currently sensing?
    • all reasoning about whatever is not immediately occurrent depends on appeal to cause/effect relation

Hume's argument against rationality of causal principle

  • Our conclusions based on the principle of cause/effect are not rational
  • Negative Argument
    • Causal knowledge is not attained via relations of ideas
      • The relation of an effect to its cause (and vice versa) can never be attained merely by the analysis of ideas and their relations
      • No contradiction is implied by denying that some effect follows from some putative cause
      • There are no necessary connections between distinct existences
    • Causal knowledge is not attained via a rational process of induction from cases
      • Hume denies that there is any rational process of arriving at a causal conclusion based on reasoning
        1. In the past when I've had caffeine I have felt more alert
        2. therefore when I have this caffeine I will feel more alert
      • We have no defensible rational justification for believing (2) on the basis of (1)
  • Positive Argument
    • Custom or Habit
    • Redefinition of 'cause'
